Brick 1 — What Is an LLM?

LLM stands for Large Language Model. Forget the jargon. Here is what it actually is.

What it learned

An LLM was trained on an enormous amount of text — books, articles, websites, code, conversations. Not to memorise it. To learn the patterns of human language and reasoning. It learned how ideas connect. How sentences are structured. How problems are solved in writing.

What it does

Given text as input, it predicts what text should come next — but with such sophistication that the output appears to reason, explain, create, and decide. It does not think the way humans do. But the output is indistinguishable from thinking for most practical purposes.

🏛

The most important thing to understand about an LLM: It has no memory between conversations. Every time you start a new chat, it wakes up knowing nothing about you. It is like a brilliant professor with amnesia — extraordinary knowledge, zero personal history with you. This is the root of almost every "AI problem" people encounter. And it is the problem that the rest of this document explains how to solve.

Claude is Anthropic's LLM. ChatGPT is OpenAI's. Gemini is Google's. They are all LLMs — different training, different personalities, different strengths. This document is written for Claude specifically, but the concepts apply to all of them.

Brick 2 — The Difference Between Chatting and Building

Most people interact with AI in one of two ways. Understanding the difference between them is the single biggest conceptual leap in this document.

LEVEL 1 — USING AI

Asking questions

You open Claude. You type a question. Claude answers. You read the answer. You close the tab. Next time you come back, Claude remembers nothing.

This is like calling a helpline. The person on the other end is smart and helpful. But they don't know you. Every call starts from zero.

LEVEL 2 — BUILDING WITH AI

Deploying systems

You give Claude a persistent identity, a knowledge base, a set of rules, and connections to your real data. It acts on your behalf — continuously, accurately, without you present.

This is like hiring a specialist employee, training them thoroughly, and giving them access to your systems. They show up every day already knowing everything relevant.

This entire document is about Level 2. The restaurant analogy, the massage parlour, the token economics, the Projects and Skills — all of it is about moving from "asking questions" to "deploying intelligent systems." The concepts are not hard. The shift in thinking is the work.

Brick 5 — What Are Skills, Agents, and RAG? (Plain English)

📋

A Skill

A reusable document — written in plain English — that tells an AI how to behave in a specific domain. It encodes your rules, your business logic, your non-negotiables, and pointers to the data it should use.

Think of it as the training manual you give a new specialist employee on their first day — except this employee reads it perfectly and follows it exactly, every single time.

🤖

An Agent

An agent is an LLM given tools and a reasoning loop. It does not just answer — it acts. It can call APIs, query databases, run code, search the web, and loop through multiple steps to complete a task autonomously.

Think of it as the difference between asking a colleague a question and delegating a project. An agent is delegation, not conversation.

🔍

RAG

Retrieval-Augmented Generation. When a question comes in, the system searches a large knowledge base (documents, transcripts, research papers) and retrieves the most relevant pieces. Those pieces get injected into Claude's context before it answers.

Think of it as the sommelier — the AI does not carry the entire wine cellar in its head. It goes and fetches the right bottle when asked.

Brick 6 — What Is MCP and Why Does It Matter?

MCP stands for Model Context Protocol. Anthropic open-sourced it. In plain English: it is the standardised way for AI systems to connect to external data and tools — so you build the integration once and any AI can use it.

Without MCP

Every AI tool builds its own custom connection to every data source. Claude connects to your database one way. ChatGPT connects another way. OpenClaw connects a third way. All custom, all fragile, all break when anything changes.

It is like every appliance in your house having a different plug shape. You need an adapter for each one.

With MCP

You build one MCP server that wraps your data source. Any MCP-compatible AI can connect to it using the same standard. Change the AI, keep the server. The integration never breaks.

It is USB-C for AI. One standard, every device.

🔑

The most important thing about MCP: The MCP server is where your data governance lives. It decides what the AI can and cannot access. It enforces read-only. It logs every query. It prevents PII from leaking. Without MCP, the AI has direct access to everything — and that is a problem. With MCP, access is controlled, audited, and reversible.

A walled memory space inside Claude.ai. Everything discussed here persists between sessions. Claude remembers your terminology, your decisions, your preferences. Files you upload are always available.

Use it for: An ongoing area of work — BaySt analytics, Healthspan build, personal health. One project per domain.

Like having a permanent office where your colleague works — they know this space, they remember everything discussed here.
SKILL

The Training Manual

A reusable document that encodes domain expertise — rules, reasoning steps, data sources, constraints. It can be loaded into any project, any conversation, any platform. Write once, use everywhere.

Use it for: Deep domain specialisation that repeats across many conversations. Your health protocols. Your BaySt analytics methodology.

Like the training manual you give a specialist — they read it once, follow it always, and can move between offices without losing the expertise.
Together: A Project is the office. A Skill is the specialist knowledge. You deploy a Skill inside a Project to get an AI that both knows its environment (Project) and has deep expertise (Skill). This is the combination that makes the difference.

Chapter I — "How does the massage therapist know about the meal?"
01 — Cross-System Context

The Marathon Customer
Visits the Massage Parlour

This question cuts to the heart of one of the most important and misunderstood problems in AI system design. AI systems do not share context automatically. They are isolated by default. The restaurant has no idea the customer is going to the massage parlour. The massage parlour has no idea the customer just ate.

But the customer carries context on their body. They are bloated. They ate pre-marathon. They are about to run 42km. The question is: how does that context get from their body into the massage therapist's decision-making?

This is the exact same problem as: how does your Healthspan daily briefing agent know what decisions were made in yesterday's bloodwork chat? How does the BaySt EDA agent know what the team decided about data governance in this morning's meeting?

🎯

The core insight: Context can travel in exactly three ways. On the customer (they carry it). Through a shared system (a record that both places can read). Or handed off explicitly (the restaurant calls ahead). Each maps precisely to an AI architecture pattern.

The Three Ways Context Travels

METHOD 1 — The Customer Carries It (Context in the Prompt)

The customer walks into the massage parlour and says: "I just ate a large pre-marathon meal 45 minutes ago — protein-heavy, some complex carbs. I'm running in 2 hours. Please adjust your approach accordingly."

The customer is the context carrier. They bring their own history into the new system explicitly. In AI terms: the user or skill injects relevant context at the start of each new conversation. This is Mode C in your health skill — you load the reference files that represent "what the customer knows about themselves."

→ In your health skill: SKILL.md loads bloodwork-timeline.md + decisions-log.md at session start
→ The AI begins every conversation already knowing your full health history
→ Limitation: only as current as the last time you updated the reference files

METHOD 2 — A Shared Record Both Can Read (Shared Database / MCP)

The restaurant and the massage parlour both connect to a shared health record system — like a hotel concierge card that travels with the guest. When the customer checks into the massage parlour, the therapist pulls up the record: "Meal logged 45 minutes ago. Marathon in 2 hours. Avoid deep tissue on abdomen."

In AI terms: both systems connect to the same database via MCP. The restaurant's agent wrote the meal record. The massage parlour's agent reads it. The context lives in one place. Both systems query it on demand. This is Mode A in your health skill — live Supabase queries.

→ In BaySt: the analytics DB is the shared record. Every agent reads from the same source.
→ Your decisions-log.md is a file-based version of this — written by one chat, read by all others
→ This is the architecture you want for Phase 0.5 onwards

METHOD 3 — Explicit Handoff (Agent-to-Agent Passing)

The restaurant agent finishes its job and produces a structured output: "Meal summary: 1,240 kcal, 68g protein, 140g carbs. Consumed 45 min ago. Next destination: massage. Flag: high-fibre meal, risk of GI discomfort during prone position." This summary is passed directly to the massage parlour's intake agent.

In AI terms: one agent's output becomes the next agent's input. The orchestrator agent collects context from the restaurant system and injects it into the massage system's prompt as a structured handoff note. This is how sub-agent pipelines work — each agent produces a structured output that feeds the next.

→ In your health skill: conversation close protocol writes decisions to decisions-log.md
→ Next session starts by reading that log — Method 3 handoff via filesystem
→ In BaySt: EDA agent output (quality score + clarification questions) feeds acquisition decision agent

The Context Passport

Think of it this way. The customer carries a Context Passport — a structured document that travels with them between systems. Every system they interact with can stamp it and read it. The massage therapist doesn't need the full restaurant order — just the relevant facts that affect their job.

The Oil Dispenser — MCP in the Massage Parlour

You mentioned the massage MCP specifically — an exact amount of oil based on some formula. This is a perfect illustration of what an MCP tool looks like in a non-tech domain.

The Tool Definition

Tool name: calculate_oil_dose
Inputs: body_weight_kg, treatment_area, skin_type, session_duration_min
Output: oil_ml (numeric)

The Governance Rule

The therapist agent calls the tool. The tool runs the formula. The tool dispenses exactly the calculated amount. The therapist cannot override the formula — they can only input the parameters.

The Audit Log

Every dispense: therapist ID, client ID, calculation inputs, ml dispensed, timestamp. Inventory automatically decremented. Billing automatically calculated. No manual entry.

This is identical to your BaySt cost-of-collections formula. The agent calls the Python function. The function runs. The number comes back. The agent never does the arithmetic itself. The MCP server is the oil dispenser — it enforces the calculation, logs the dispense, and the agent cannot bypass it.

Chapter II — "What is a token and why should I care about bloat?"
02 — Token Economics

Tokens, Context Windows,
& The Bloat Problem

This is the economics of AI. Every conversation has a budget. Tokens are the currency. When you run out of budget, the AI starts forgetting things. When your budget is wasted on the wrong things, the AI gives you worse answers even though it technically has room.

What Is a Token?

📝

Not a Word

A token is roughly ¾ of a word. "marathon" = 2 tokens. "I am running a marathon" = 5 tokens. Punctuation counts. Spaces count.

💰

It's the Unit of Cost

You pay per token — both what you send in (input) and what Claude writes back (output). Opus costs more per token than Sonnet. Sonnet more than Haiku.

🪟

The Window Is Finite

Claude Opus 4.6 has ~1 million tokens. Sounds enormous. But you can fill it fast with long conversations, large documents, and verbose system prompts.

What's Inside the Context Window Right Now

Every time Claude responds, it "sees" everything in its context window simultaneously. Think of it as a whiteboard that holds all the relevant information. Here's what's on that whiteboard in a typical Healthspan session:

The Bloat Problem — When Multiple Threads Go Wrong

Here's where your instinct is exactly right. If you have 5 concurrent health chats, each with long histories, each loading large reference files, and you keep asking follow-up questions — you start filling the window with the wrong things. The AI starts "forgetting" earlier context or producing worse answers because its working memory is cluttered.

At 93%+ used, Claude starts truncating earlier conversation history to make room. You lose continuity. Answers get worse. This is the bloat problem.

♻️

The Rolling Summary Solution — This is exactly why your Healthspan skill has brain_thread_summary_after = 5 in system_config. After 5 back-and-forths, instead of keeping the full conversation history, the skill compresses it into a dense summary. The summary takes 300 tokens. The full history would have taken 3,000. You retain the meaning. You shed the weight.

The Six Rules of Token Hygiene

These are the practical habits that separate people who get consistent, high-quality AI output from people who get degraded answers as sessions go long.

RULE WHAT TO DO WHY IT MATTERS
One topic per conversation Don't ask about testosterone AND food AND training in one thread. Use separate focused conversations. Each conversation loads only the relevant reference files. Keeps history clean. Context stays relevant.
Capture and close At the end of each conversation, capture decisions to the log. Then close the conversation. Start fresh next time. A 2-hour conversation accumulates huge history. Starting fresh with a captured summary is always cleaner.
Never paste full documents Don't paste your entire bloodwork PDF into a chat. Upload it as a file or use a reference file system. A 27-page PDF pasted as text can consume 40,000+ tokens instantly. Files are handled more efficiently.
Use precise questions "What is my HbA1c trend since 2020 and what does it mean for my marathon goal?" beats "Tell me about my health." Vague questions generate verbose answers that fill history. Precise questions get precise answers.
Trust the system prompt Don't re-explain your context in every message. Your skill already loaded it. The AI already knows. Re-explaining wastes tokens on information already in the context. Trust what the skill loaded.
Use the right model for the right task Simple lookups → Sonnet or Haiku. Deep analysis, complex reasoning → Opus. Don't burn Opus on "what did I eat yesterday?" Cost discipline. Opus at ~15x the cost of Haiku. Match intelligence to complexity.
🧮

Quick Token Maths for BaySt: Your BaySt analyst skill system prompt = ~2,000 tokens. Schema semantic layer = ~3,000 tokens. A typical query answer = ~500 tokens. 10 back-and-forths = ~5,000 tokens of history. Total session: ~10,500 tokens. At Sonnet rates (~$3/M tokens), a full team session costs ~$0.03. Running 100 sessions a month costs ~$3. This is not a cost problem. It is a quality problem — keeping context clean so the AI stays sharp throughout.

How to Structure Your Projects — The Right Topology

⚠️

Skills — The Reusable Intelligence Layer

A Skill is the recipe book. A Project is the restaurant. You can use the same recipe book in multiple restaurants. You can have multiple recipe books in one restaurant. They are separate things that work together.

✓ WHAT SKILLS ARE FOR
  • Encoding deep domain expertise that gets reused across many conversations
  • Defining non-negotiable rules that must always apply (never skip Viome check)
  • Building reusable reasoning patterns (the STEP sequence in your health skill)
  • Creating specialist agents that non-technical team members can invoke without understanding the internals
  • Storing institutional knowledge that outlives any single conversation
  • Packaging a workflow so new team members get it instantly on first use
✗ WHAT SKILLS ARE NOT FOR
  • One-off questions you'll never repeat
  • General knowledge Claude already has (history, science, public information)
  • Storing live data (use a database for that, not a skill)
  • Replacing actual governance (skills are instructions, not enforcement)
  • Building skills before you know what the repeated workflow actually is — validate manually first, then encode

The Professional Workflow — How to Use Both Together

1
Start with a Project, no Skill
Open a new Claude project. Write basic custom instructions — who you are, what this is, key terminology. Start having conversations. Explore the domain. Don't build a skill yet. You don't know what you need.
2
Notice what you repeat
After 2–3 weeks of daily use, you'll see patterns. "I always have to remind it about my Viome food rules." "I always have to tell it to use three-tier biomarker resolution." "I always have to explain the recovery rate formula." These repetitions are your skill content.
3
Extract repeating patterns into a Skill
Write a SKILL.md. Encode the repeating instructions. Add the reference files that always need to be loaded. Define the non-negotiable rules. This is exactly what you did with pc-health-intelligence — you validated manually for 3 weeks, then encoded the pattern.
4
Test the Skill against your hardest questions
Give it a question that previously required lots of manual context-setting. Does it get it right first time? Does it load the right reference files? Does it apply the right constraints? Fix what fails. This is your negative testing.
5
Automate the Skill's maintenance
Set up a process to keep reference files current. Your nightly Cowork build is the gold standard. At minimum, update reference files whenever significant new data arrives. A stale skill is worse than no skill — it gives confident wrong answers.
6
Share the Skill, not just the Project
When a new team member joins, give them the skill file. They get the full institutional intelligence immediately — they don't have to build up context over weeks of conversations. The skill is the onboarding document and the operating system simultaneously.

05 Capture decisions, close conversations Context built up in a long session should be preserved in a decisions log, not left to decay in a conversation history that eventually gets truncated. Good analysis gets lost. Team repeats the same analysis next week. Institutional knowledge evaporates.
