Case Study — Voice AI

From Problem to Production in 96 Hours

How a founder-led team, one shared office, and a bias for action built an AI-powered agent training system over a single working week — and what it says about the future of debt resolution in India.

By the BaySys.ai Team March 2026 18 min read

India has a $48 billion distressed consumer debt problem — and the industry built to solve it is, for the most part, doing it wrong.

The pool is growing at 1.5 times annually. Projected to exceed $70 billion by 2027. Sitting inside a $1.2 trillion consumer credit market with over 400 million Indians active in the credit bureau. By any measure, this is one of the largest unsolved asset recovery challenges in any emerging economy.

The conventional approach is to hire people and make calls. An operation managing the size of assets BaySys.ai manages would, by industry benchmarks, employ 300 to 400 people. BaySys.ai does it with 16 at headquarters. That difference — not a rounding error, a structural one — is the thesis made visible.
Baysys.ai with its capital partners is one of the largest retail stressed asset balance sheets in India.

When BaySys.ai's founding team began acquiring distressed consumer debt portfolios, they arrived with a clear and contrarian reading of the market. The obvious thesis — hire field agents, maximise call volume, extract payments through persistence — was not the one they believed in.

The Dormant 80%

The industry operates on an assumption: roughly 8 to 15 per cent of a late-stage distressed portfolio is contactable and collectible in any given cycle. Everyone prices portfolios on this assumption. The remaining 75 to 80 per cent is not written off — it is simply treated as worth nothing.

The BaySys.ai's team looked at that 80 per cent and saw something different. Not a write-off, but an option. A person who defaulted two years ago is not the same person today. Life events happen — a new job, a relative's financial assistance, a changed personal situation, a regulatory pressure the customer had not previously understood. The 80 per cent that looks dormant today is a population in motion. The question is not whether anyone in it will ever pay. It is whether, when they are ready, you are the organisation they can find and trust.

That requires not harassment at scale, but intelligent, trust-building engagement at cost low enough that you can hold on for decades, automated enough to be economical across hundreds of thousands of accounts, personal enough to convert when the moment arrives.

The Digital Channel Insight

India's distressed consumer debt at this stage of the market is not made up of borrowers who walked into a bank branch. They were onboarded digitally — through lending apps, NBFC platforms, fintech products, and embedded credit facilities. They opened accounts on their phones. They never set foot in a bank.

Calling these customers with a human agent using a script written for branch-originated borrowers is a categorical mismatch. The customer expects digital engagement because that is how the relationship began. An AI voice system that reaches them on the same device, with the same level of convenience they used when they borrowed, is not a compromise on the customer experience. It is the correct customer experience.

The Problem

5 PM, Monday. A signal in the data.

BaySys.ai had been running AI-led introductory calls across its customer base to establish a foundational metric: contactability. An AI voice bot — not a human agent, but an automated system that introduced BaySys.ai, verified identity, and asked a simple question — was calling customers across different segments of the 650,000-account portfolio.

The headline result was striking. Across the full base, roughly 40 per cent of customers picked up the phone. But raw pickup rates are noisy. Robocalls get answered and hung up in under three seconds. The team cut the data by call duration: calls under ten seconds were discarded as non-contacts and re-run. What remained — calls where a customer had engaged with the bot for more than ten seconds — was the real signal.

In the paid prime segment — approximately 15,000 customers who had previously made at least one payment to BaySys.ai — this cleaner dataset identified around 4,000 live, engaged contacts. These were customers who had heard the bot, stayed on the line, and interacted. They were, by any reasonable measure, reachable and potentially willing to engage.

These 4,000 were moved to manual calling. Human agents were assigned the list. The expectation was a conversion rate at least consistent with the portfolio's baseline. What came back was worse — meaningfully worse.

The team did what experienced operators do when data surprises them. They went back to the source. A listening session was convened at 5 PM on a Monday. The collections head, the CEO, and members of the product team sat together and listened to fifty sample calls.

What they heard was not a data problem. It was a training problem — and a deeper one than anyone had anticipated.

"Customers were suspicious this was a scam. Agents couldn't explain why the originating bank had sold the account to a debt buyer — or why BaySys.ai, as the collection servicer, had the right to call them. They were losing the customer in the first ninety seconds."

BaySys.ai's role in this chain matters for context. When a bank sells a distressed loan portfolio, it sells to a debt buyer — a licensed entity that acquires the underlying receivables. BaySys.ai is the collection servicer: the entity that calls customers on the debt buyer's behalf.

This is a legally precise and regulatorily significant arrangement — but to a customer receiving a call out of the blue, it is bewildering. "Why are you calling me? My loan was with Portfolio 3." An agent who cannot explain this chain clearly enough to be credible will lose the call every time.

These were digitally-onboarded borrowers. They were comfortable questioning a caller's authority. They had likely seen warnings about financial scams. The agent who could not explain the mechanics of debt assignment was indistinguishable, to the customer, from a fraudster.

The immediate operational reality was brutal. Every further call on the paid prime base was burning a customer relationship that was difficult to rebuild. The correct decision — the only defensible decision — was to stop all calling on these accounts immediately. But stopping calling means collections goes to zero. The problem required a solution that was both fast and good.

The Decision

How do you train 250 agents overnight?

The question the team faced at 5 PM on Monday was deceptively simple to state and extraordinarily difficult to answer. Traditional agent training works like this: a trainer works one-on-one or in small groups, walks through scripts, roleplays customer scenarios, scores performance manually, and gives feedback. For 250 agents, spread across multiple agencies and geographies, this would take weeks — possibly months at the quality level required. Weeks was not an option.

What was needed was a system that could simulate a realistic customer conversation, adapt its difficulty to challenge the agent, score performance automatically against a defined rubric, and do all of this over a normal phone line — the same GSM/PSTN infrastructure that agents use every day. An agent training on a web interface is not training for the real environment. If the system could not operate convincingly over a mobile call, it was not a training system.

The founding partner with technical responsibility — who had been a hands-on developer and systems architect for 25 years across financial services and had personally architected the CRM these agents used every day — spent the hours between 5 PM and 10 PM on Monday doing what experienced technologists do before committing to a build: exhaustive research. Using AI tools to accelerate the process, he personally led a deep technical analysis of the voice AI stack. He would be referred to as the CTO from this point forward, because that is the function he performs — though at a 16-person company with a flat structure, the more accurate description is the founding partner who does everything, including this.

The Technical Constraint Nobody Talks About

The research surfaced a finding that changed the entire evaluation framework. Most comparisons of text-to-speech providers are done in browser environments, measuring audio quality at 24kHz or higher. But PSTN telephony — the phone network — operates at 8kHz. A premium TTS engine that produces beautiful, natural-sounding speech at 24kHz has that audio downsampled before it reaches the caller's ear. The quality gap between premium and functional narrows dramatically once you account for the transmission medium.

A second, equally important constraint emerged: language. And this one is specific enough to be worth explaining in full.

The "mein/MAIN" problem — why it matters

In spoken Hindi, मैं (mein) means "I." It is one of the most frequent words in any Hinglish conversation. Agents use it constantly: main XYZ bol raha hoon (I am XYZ speaking), main samjha sakta hoon (I can explain this). It is the first word of almost every agent self-introduction.

Global English-trained TTS models see this transliterated as "mein" and pronounce it to rhyme with the English word "rain" — MAIN. To a native Hindi speaker, this sounds wrong the moment they hear it. Not subtly wrong. Jarringly wrong. It is the acoustic equivalent of a foreign accent so strong it signals immediately that the voice is neither Indian nor trained on Indian speech. No amount of otherwise natural delivery can recover from this. The customer's trust is gone before the introduction is finished.

This single phonetic failure was the hardest filter to pass in the TTS evaluation — and it eliminated multiple otherwise well-reviewed providers instantly, regardless of their quality on other dimensions.

The fix — discovered during fine-tuning: using देवनागरी (Devanagari) script directly in the system prompt for Hindi words forces the TTS engine to process the correct phoneme, bypassing the Latin-script mispronunciation entirely. Writing मैं instead of "mein" in the prompt produces correct Hindi pronunciation. Non-obvious, and only discovered after live testing — but it made a material difference to the system's credibility on first listen.

The same principle extends to every regional language in the library. The Tamil-speaking persona (Karthikeyan) uses words like seri (okay), illa (no), aiyyo (exasperation) — common in Tamil-Hinglish conversation. Written in Roman script, ElevenLabs reads them as English and mispronounces every one. Written in Tamil script — சரி, இல்ல, ஐயோ — the engine handles them correctly. The rule is consistent: wherever a regional language word appears in a spoken response, it should be written in its native script, not transliterated.

The second major decision was whether to build the orchestration layer from scratch — custom WebSocket handling, turn-taking logic, telephony integration, post-call extraction — or to use an existing voice AI platform. Building from scratch offered maximum control but would take a week minimum to reach production quality.

By 10 PM, the architectural decision was made: Bolna.ai as the orchestration layer.

On choosing an orchestration platform over building raw

Selecting Bolna.ai was a deliberate trade-off, entered into with clear eyes. The team understood two things going in. First, using an orchestration layer means paying a margin on top of the underlying AI provider costs — you are not calling Deepgram, Gemini, and Cartesia directly at their API rates. Second, routing audio through an additional infrastructure layer adds latency. Every audio packet travels to Bolna.ai's servers before being distributed to the STT, LLM, and TTS providers and returned. This is measurable overhead, not theoretical.

Both costs were accepted because the alternative — building WebSocket audio handling, PSTN integration, turn-taking logic, session management, and post-call extraction from scratch — was estimated at a minimum of one week's work. In a crisis with collections at zero, that week did not exist. The premium paid to Bolna.ai was, in every meaningful sense, the cost of compressing seven days into one night.

The Build

10 PM Monday to 4 AM Tuesday: The First Working System

What the CTO began building at 10 PM on Monday was not a prototype. It was a production-grade AI trainer designed to do one thing with extraordinary precision: simulate a real Indian debt resolution call, at every difficulty level, with a scoring engine that could tell an agent exactly where they failed and why.

The system was built around a set of carefully engineered customer personas — each representing a real archetype from BaySys.ai's portfolio. Not generic characters, but fully specified individuals: age, occupation, city, loan product, days past due, previous contact history, language preference, and a defined psychological profile describing how they would respond to different agent approaches.

Each persona was configured with five escalation levels — L1 through L5 — where L1 represented a cooperative customer nearly ready to pay, and L5 represented a hostile customer convinced the entire call was a scam, weaponising every ambiguity against the agent. The L5 persona was designed to break agents who were not fully prepared. That was the point.

Critically, the difficulty level is invisible to the agent. The system manages escalation automatically based on progression history — rewarding consistent performers with harder scenarios, keeping struggling agents at recoverable difficulty until they build core skills. An agent placing a training call has no idea whether they are about to speak with a cooperative customer or a hostile one. Real customers do not announce their mood.

The Validation

10:30 AM Tuesday: The Room Makes the Decision

By 10:30 AM, the company had reviewed the recording and the scores independently and assembled to decide what to do next. The CTO raised two concerns. The first was latency: the round-trip delay between the agent's speech and the AI's response was noticeable. The second was language quality: while Cartesia had cleared the mein/MAIN test, there were edge cases where the Hinglish phonetics needed further tuning.

The rest of the team addressed both. On latency, the context was different: this was a training system, not a customer-facing one. The agent knew they were speaking to an AI. Acceptable latency was a wider window than a live customer call would demand. On language quality, the team agreed the pronunciation was sufficiently natural for the training purpose, with phonetic fine-tuning as a fast follow.

The decision: proceed to pilot.

"The speed was possible because the right people were in the same room. Not on a call. Not over Slack. In the same room, looking at the same screen, having the same argument."

The 5 PM to 4 AM sprint, and the 10:30 AM decision that followed, happened in a single shared physical office. The team — including co-founders with decades of experience in exactly this domain — could ask each other questions and get answers in seconds. They could pull up the same recording, listen together, disagree, and resolve the disagreement before it became a blocker. Remote teams build things. Co-located high-context teams build things fast and build them right.

The Full Training Persona Library — 9 AI Debtors

With the go-ahead given, the next task was to expand the system from a single persona to a full library. This required a dedicated tool — and the CTO had anticipated it. Before writing a single persona specification, he had built what the team calls the Persona Spawner: a purpose-built script that generates the complete persona configuration matrix, formats each persona as a structured Bolna.ai-compatible specification, and uploads the full set programmatically to the platform's API. The Persona Spawner was the first thing built after the architectural decision. Without a systematic generation tool, expanding from one persona to nine would have required configuring each agent profile by hand inside the Bolna.ai interface — hours of error-prone manual work for what should be a scripted operation. With it, the full variant matrix was generated and deployed in two to three hours. By 12:30 PM on Tuesday, six personas were live. The library expanded to nine by end of day.

The Debug Loop

Generating a Persona Takes Minutes. Making It Behave Takes Days.

The Persona Spawner generates the specification. What it cannot do is verify that the AI behaves correctly once the specification is deployed to Bolna.ai and a real caller starts talking. That verification required a different kind of work — methodical, iterative, and considerably less glamorous than the build narrative suggests.

Every test call through the system produces two outputs. The first is what any participant would expect: a voice conversation. The second is what made this testing methodology unusually rigorous — a structured JSON file containing the full call transcript, the 28-point scorecard with each criterion's result, the AI's extracted metadata (bank name, product type, difficulty level), a free-text coaching note, and a call outcome classification. This machine-readable artifact of every call made it possible to do something that most teams do not do: treat prompt debugging as engineering work, not guesswork.

The CTO uploaded the JSON transcript from each test call into a dedicated testing session and analysed it line by line — not listening to audio, but reading the AI's exact output against what the persona specification required. The output is exact and unambiguous. A call that scores 20 out of 28 leaves a record of precisely which 4 criteria failed, in which domain, with a coaching note generated in the same pass:

What emerged from reading these artifacts systematically was a taxonomy of failure modes that could not have been anticipated from the prompt text alone:

Loop bug Scripted example lines in a section became a script. The AI repeated the same response verbatim two or three times when the caller kept pressing.
Section skip A section requiring 2–3 conversational turns was compressed into a single half-sentence, then the persona jumped forward. The emotional arc collapsed.
Role confusion A role-integrity guardrail ("you are the customer, not the agent") misfired on legitimate caller statements, producing an unnatural preamble mid-conversation.
Company-name bridge When an agent mentioned the originating bank but not the debt buyer's name, the persona had no defined behaviour — it guessed, inconsistently.
Hindi naturalness Specific Hinglish constructions that sounded grammatically correct in text were unnatural in speech. Required word-by-word correction, not structural rewriting.
Product confusion A persona using the word "loan" when the caller had opened with "credit card." Caught immediately by a native speaker; would not appear in automated text scoring.

Each failure produced exactly one fix. Not a structural rewrite — a targeted change to the specific instruction or example that caused the problem. The version was incremented. The call was run again. The fix was confirmed or revised. This is the work that prompt engineering actually is, when it is done with the discipline of a software engineering process rather than the intuition of a content editor.

v8.4
BugProduct awareness + closing repetition. Product-specific instruction added to Phase 1 (loan vs. credit card). Section 5 and Section 6 closings differentiated to prevent duplicate Ramesh deferral.
v8.5
BugName-confusion absorption fix. One sentence added: even if the caller explains the company name in the same breath, the persona does not absorb the explanation — it stays on the unfamiliar name for the full Phase 1 response. Realistic: a person latches on to a strange word and misses the rest of the sentence.

With one persona validated to v8.5, the question became: how do you validate the other eight without making eighteen more phone calls? Eighteen calls — nine personas, two products each — at five to six minutes apiece is two hours of manual testing. And it only catches the bugs that surface in any given run of that specific call.

The answer was to separate the voice layer from the behaviour layer. Every failure mode being hunted — loops, section skips, role confusion — is an LLM output problem. The same model that runs inside Bolna.ai can be called directly via API. The CTO built a Python regression harness: load a persona prompt, inject a difficulty level, replay a hand-crafted caller script turn by turn, record every response as raw JSON. No phone call. No audio.

Six scripts, each targeting a distinct failure mode:

Script 01 · Positive control The ideal agent Full journey in sequence. Baseline — if a persona fails this, the prompt is fundamentally broken.
Script 02 · Adversarial Scam-heavy caller Three fraud challenges before anything else. Tests emotional hold under sustained trust pressure.
Script 03 · Stress test Weak agent No intro — straight to payment demand. Tests character hold when the caller gives the persona nothing to work with.
Script 04 · Guard test Company name withheld Caller circles the company name for five turns without stating it. Debtor must hold in the trust-challenge phase — premature advancement is a failure.
Scripts 05a & 05b · Exception test Devanagari name input Turn 1 contains the debtor's name in Devanagari — exactly as Deepgram transcribes it from audio. Persona must respond normally, not loop on its own name.
Script 06 · Character test Misgendering throughout Caller addresses female personas as "sir" for the entire call. Correct or ignore — but don't break character.

The run model is GPT-4.1-mini — identical to production, 360 times per full sweep. The analysis pass uses Claude Opus 4.6 with extended thinking: prompt debugging is a deep but finite analytical problem, and it runs once per batch, not per conversation. A batch runner handles the full matrix in ~35 minutes with a --dry-run flag to preview scope before spending credits. Script 04 was the highest-signal run — it caught the premature trigger immediately, a one-line fix applied across all thirteen files. Scripts 05a/b confirmed the Devanagari exception clause. Script 06 passed clean, no change needed.

The scale difference is worth stating plainly. 360 test combinations at five minutes each is 30 hours of phone calls. The harness runs them in 35 minutes. But the more important point is that the harness existed at all before go-live. Most builders would ship, wait for something to break in production, and fix it then. Building a regression harness before the system is live — covering known failure modes, edge cases, and persona-specific exceptions — is the kind of foresight that only comes from having seen what production incidents look like. It is not on any AI tutorial checklist. It comes from two decades of making systems production-ready.

With the testing infrastructure in place, the Persona Spawner changes character entirely. It is no longer just a tool for generating nine personas. It is a production line. The Spawner generates the specification, the testing script validates it against the full battery of known failure patterns, and the agent is deployed to Bolna.ai's API — all programmatically. A persona that would have taken hours to configure, test by phone call, debug, and redeploy can now go from specification to validated deployment in minutes. The same process that produced nine personas can produce a hundred. Different banks, different products, different emotional archetypes, different regional languages — each one generated from the same template logic, each one tested against the same LLM that will run it in production (currently GPT-4.1-mini on Azure), each one deployed without a human touching the Bolna.ai interface.

Why this matters beyond this build

The structured JSON output that made this debugging workflow possible is not incidental — it was an explicit design requirement. A voice AI system that produces only audio is auditable only by listening. A system that produces a timestamped, structured record of every call — transcript, score, metadata, outcome — is auditable at scale. That distinction becomes significant the moment you consider running this not as a training system for 250 agents, but as a customer-facing system operating across 650,000 accounts.

The Testing

25 Calls, Two Scorers, One Question: Does the Machine Agree with the Human?

The critical validation question was not whether the system worked technically. It was whether the AI's scoring judgement matched an experienced human's. A system that gives inconsistent or arbitrary scores is worse than no system — it trains agents against a rubric they cannot trust.

The team ran approximately 25 to 30 phone calls through the system. The CEO scored every single call personally, with a pen and paper, before looking at the JSON output. A second evaluator scored cold, without prior context. The result: 98% agreement between the CEO's manual scores and the AI's output across ten validated calls.

The Economics

8 Cents a Minute: The Cost of Scale

During the testing phase, the team ran a parallel cost analysis. Voice AI orchestration involves multiple metered services: the LLM, the STT engine, and the TTS engine — each provider charges differently. Configurations were evaluated from 15 cents per minute at the premium end to 7 cents per minute using more economical choices. The optimal solution on pure quality metrics cost 15 cents per minute. By accepting approximately 100 milliseconds of additional latency and making specific STT/TTS choices tuned for 8kHz deployment, the team landed at 8 cents per minute with no voice quality compromise that mattered in deployment.

The CRM Integration

Tuesday Night: The Part Nobody Sees

By Tuesday evening, the standalone trainer was validated and the manual pilot was underway: agents dialling in to the system via the Bolna.ai interface, receiving their callback from the AI trainer, scores coming back clean. The system was working.

But a manual pilot through a standalone interface is not a production system. Production means integration into the CRM that every agent opens at the start of their shift. It means the training workflow is the first thing an agent does before touching a live customer account — not an optional exercise accessed via a separate link. It means the system decides, based on the warm-up scores, whether the agent is qualified to proceed to live calls or should be held back.

Here is the context that makes what follows remarkable. BaySys.ai runs four engineers across its entire technology operation. Those four engineers are responsible for the CRM managing $700 million in live assets, the data pipelines feeding portfolio analytics, the agent management tooling, and the day-to-day operational infrastructure of a 16-person company managing 650,000 accounts. This is not a team with spare capacity. Each engineer is running at full utilisation on systems that cannot be deprioritised. When a crisis arrives, there is no one to redirect. The CTO could not tap the engineering team for this build — the engineering team was already keeping the operation alive. The build had to be done by the one person who both understood the architecture and had time that could be temporarily reprioritised: himself.

He had been awake for over 40 hours. He had personally built and configured the Bolna.ai trainer from scratch overnight on Monday. He was 45 years old and had not personally written a module of this size in over a decade. He had been travelling continuously for a month.

He decided to sleep.

This was the correct decision. Over the past month, the CTO had built out a comprehensive AI development environment: Claude Code configured with deep context about the BaySys.ai codebase, a skills library for schema documentation and project management artefacts, and a set of code review agents that enforced quality standards without requiring human supervision of every line. When he started writing the CRM integration module at 6 AM on Wednesday morning — rested, with clear context — he was working with an AI pair-programmer that understood the BaySys.ai architecture, the build rules, and the integration constraints.

By 2 PM, the standalone module was complete: 129 tests passing, zero ruff findings, a Django app that plugged into the CRM with three backend changes and five React component additions.

The finished system — Django backend, React UI, Bolna voice integration, CRM adapter layer, and an automated regression harness that runs 360 test combinations against GPT-4.1-mini with a Claude Opus 4.6 analysis pass — comes to roughly 16,000 lines across Python, TypeScript, and documentation, verified by 152 automated tests. A team of four or five developers would quote six to eight weeks to build an equivalent — and that assumes they ship clean. This was done in 96 hours, by one person, start to finish, including the refinements.

The Lesson

What This Actually Proves

It would be easy to read this story as a story about AI. About a clever technology that compressed a weeks-long project into four days. And that reading is not wrong — the tools mattered enormously. Bolna.ai as an orchestration layer, Claude Code as an AI pair-programmer, the research and reasoning tools that compressed five hours of architectural analysis into an evening — without these, the timeline was not possible.

But the more important story is about what AI requires to work at this level. The technology did not decide that the problem was worth solving at 5 PM on a Monday. It did not recognise the acoustic constraints of 8kHz PSTN telephony and understand why they changed the TTS evaluation criteria. It did not catch the mein/MAIN pronunciation failure mode that ruled out entire categories of TTS providers. It did not design a scoring rubric that mapped precisely to the failure modes of Indian collections agents handling debt assignment objections for the first time. It did not know that a 100-millisecond latency trade-off was acceptable in a training context but not in a customer-facing one.

Every one of those decisions required someone who had spent years inside this industry. The CTO who has been a hands-on developer and systems architect for 25 years in financial services. The CEO who designed the scoring rubric from his own experience listening to what breaks on a collections call. The collections head who knew exactly what his agents were failing to say. Co-founders who had built and operated the very infrastructure through which these borrowers originally took their loans.

"AI does not replace experience. It multiplies it. In the hands of people who know what they are building and why, it compresses months into days. In the hands of people who do not, it produces fast nonsense."

There is one more thing. This was done in a single shared office, by a team that has worked together for years, with the founders physically present for every key decision. The speed came from the absence of coordination friction: no ticket raised for someone in another timezone to review, no decision escalated through a management layer that did not understand the technical constraints, no alignment meeting required before a founder could make a call at 2 AM.

When experienced people who trust each other, understand the domain deeply, and are physically co-located get access to modern AI tools — this is what becomes possible. Not incremental improvement. Not a project completed slightly faster. A fundamentally different operating capability.

BaySys.ai is managing 650,000 customer accounts with 16 people at headquarters. A dormant portfolio that the market prices as worth nothing. A cost-to-collect below 29 per cent. That combination is not a limitation. It is the proof.
