Can AI Understand Indian Phone Calls?

Hindi, Hinglish, background chaos, angry customers — real calls are messy. Your job: find out which AI combo transcribes them best and catches every compliance violation. Five vendor combinations, 50 real calls, one winner.

v7 · April 2026 · Open to all participants

1. Who is this for?

You. Whether you're an engineer, a data person, a QA analyst, or someone who's never written a line of code — you can participate. You don't need to do everything. Even if you only test the transcription (STT) part, that's a perfectly valid and complete submission. Not everyone needs to touch the LLM scoring side.

This document tells you everything you need: the vendor combinations, the scoring rubric, the scripts you'll role-play, and how we'll judge the results. A Technical Appendix at the end goes deeper for engineers.

2. The problem

Baysys.ai processes tens of thousands of customer calls every day in Hindi, English, and Hinglish. Calls range from 3 to 20 minutes. Manual listening and scoring is slow, expensive, and impossible to scale.

We need an automated Call Auditor pipeline that, for every call:

  1. Transcribes the audio into accurate text.
  2. Separates speakers — always clear who is the Agent and who is the Customer.
  3. Scores the call against the 20-parameter quality rubric (Section 4), flagging every FATAL violation.
What we are optimising for

Transcription accuracy is non-negotiable — everything downstream depends on it. For LLM scoring, a cheaper model with lower non-FATAL accuracy is acceptable, as long as it never misses a FATAL. A missed FATAL (e.g. agent discloses customer data to a third party, and the system scores it compliant) is a regulatory and reputational risk.

4. The scoring rubric

Every call is scored against the UVARCL Collections Call Quality Scorecard v2 — 19 parameters across 5 groups, maximum 100 points (weighted by group). Seven parameters carry a FATAL flag — any single FATAL = the call fails, regardless of total score.

Why FATAL detection is the primary metric

An LLM that gets non-FATAL scores slightly wrong is tolerable. An LLM that misses an agent using abusive language, sharing OTP requests, or disclosing debt to a third party is not. FATAL recall is the single most important number in this hackathon.

5. Test design

There are two evaluation streams. You can do either or both:

  1. Transcription (STT) — How accurately does each combo convert audio to diarised text? This is a perfectly complete submission on its own.
  2. LLM scoring — How reliably does each combo detect FATAL violations and score against the 19-parameter rubric?
STT-only is a valid submission

If your team focuses purely on transcription quality — comparing how well each STT provider handles Hindi, Hinglish, noise, and numbers — that is a full, scoreable submission. You do not need to test LLM scoring to participate.

Test materials provided by Baysys

  • 50 real production calls (mono MP3, 3–15 min each) — a mix of clean, moderate, and difficult calls from real collections operations.
  • 4 role-play scripts at graduated difficulty (Section 6) — you and your teammates record your own calls from these.
  • Ground truth scores — 2 experienced human auditors will score every production call against the full rubric before the hackathon. Their consensus becomes the baseline.

Blind evaluation

When rating LLM outputs, evaluators will not know which combo produced a given score. Baysys will assign shuffled labels (Combo A / B / C / D / E) per evaluator to prevent bias.

6. Role-play scripts — you make these calls

This is the fun part. You and your teammates act out these scripts and record the calls yourselves. One person plays the Agent, the other plays the Customer. Use your own phone — call each other, use speakerphone, or sit across a table.

The goal is to create realistic, messy audio that stress-tests how well each STT provider handles real Indian conditions. The more realistic, the more useful your data is.

Make it real

Have your friend stand on a busy road and play the customer. Record one call from a metro station. Do one with a ceiling fan on full speed next to the phone. Have someone talk in the background. Play a TV. The whole point is to simulate what a real collections call sounds like — not a quiet room.

Four scripts, increasing difficulty. Each one targets specific parameters from the rubric — especially FATALs. Record at least one call per script.

Script 1 — Easy (clean call, no violations)

Scenario: Agent calls about 1 overdue EMI. Proper introduction (greeting, name, UVARCL identity, bank context), RPV done, customer cooperative, PTP secured, clean closing. No FATAL violations.

What this tests: Baseline — every combo should score this correctly. Covers Introduction Quality (all 5 params ✓), Call Quality (de-escalated ✓, PTP ✓, closing ✓).

Agent:
"Namaste, mera naam Priya hai, main UVARCL se bol rahi hoon. Aapka account pehle HDFC Bank ke under tha, ab UVARCL ke paas transfer hua hai. Kya main Rajesh Kumar ji se baat kar rahi hoon? Sir, aapki date of birth confirm kar sakte hain please?"

Customer:
"Haan, 14 July 1985."

Agent:
"Thank you sir. Aapka personal loan account 4521-88-302 mein ek EMI overdue hai — seven thousand two hundred rupees, due date 15 March tha. Agar aap jaldi pay karte hain toh aage koi late charges nahi lagenge. Kya aap is week payment kar payenge?"

Customer:
"Haan Priya ji, sorry, actually salary late aayi thi. Main kal UPI se kar dunga. Amount aur details WhatsApp kar do please."

Agent:
"Zaroor sir, main aapko official UVARCL number se WhatsApp pe details bhej dungi. Toh kal, 3 April tak, seven thousand two hundred rupees UPI se — confirmed? ... Perfect. Agar koi doubt ho toh aap UVARCL ki website pe verify kar sakte hain ya hamare toll-free number pe call kar sakte hain. Thank you for your time, have a good day."

Script 2 — Medium (soft-skill failures, no FATALs)

Scenario: Agent calls about 2 overdue EMIs. Introduction is there but sloppy (no UVARCL identity, rushed RPV). Customer is irritated. Agent fumbles, uses fillers, poor objection handling. But no FATAL violations — RPV was technically done, no abuse, no TCP breach.

What this tests: Can the LLM correctly score low on fluency (#6), objection handling (#8), de-escalation (#9), and closing (#11) without over-flagging as FATAL?

Script 3 — Hard (multiple FATALs)

Scenario: Agent calls about overdue EMIs. No RPV done — dives straight into debt details. Uses abusive language when frustrated. Claims to be from the original bank (misrepresentation). Then asks customer to send payment to agent's personal UPI.

FATALs embedded: RPV ✗ (#5), Abusive language ✗ (#7), Misrepresentation ✗ (#15), Agent shared personal payment details ✗ (#18). If the LLM misses any of these, it fails.

Agent:
"Hello, main HDFC Bank se bol raha hoon." [Says HDFC, not UVARCL = Misrepresentation FATAL] "Aapka loan pe teen EMI overdue hai, total fifty two thousand baaki hai. Aapko aaj hi pay karna padega." [No RPV — jumped straight to debt details = RPV FATAL]

Customer:
"Kaun bol raha hai? Mera toh HDFC se koi loan nahi hai. Kya bakwaas hai?"

Agent:
"Sir aap jhooth bol rahe ho. System mein sab hai. Agar nahi doge toh ghar pe log aayenge."

Customer:
"Aap dhamki de rahe ho? Main complaint karunga."

Agent:
"Complaint karo, kuch nahi hoga. Tum log paise khaate ho aur phir natak karte ho, saale—" [Abusive language = Abusive Language FATAL]

Agent:
"Chalo ek kaam karo, abhi ke abhi mere UPI pe bhej do — amit.collections@paytm, main adjust kar dunga system mein." [Agent shared personal UPI for payment = Personal Payment Details FATAL]

Script 4 — Extreme (subtle FATALs hidden in an excellent call)

Scenario: Agent calls about overdue EMIs. A third party answers (customer's wife). Agent is polite, empathetic, and professional throughout — greeting, name, UVARCL identity, everything by the book. But: discloses full account details to the third party without RPV, and later asks the customer to share an OTP to "verify the payment link." The call sounds perfect.

FATALs embedded: TCP — disclosed to third party ✗ (#14), RPV ✗ (#5), OTP requested ✗ (#17). But: Greeting ✓, Self-intro ✓, ARC identity ✓, Fluency ✓, Tone ✓, Closing ✓. This tests whether the LLM can spot FATALs buried inside an otherwise excellent call.

Recording tips

  • Format: Mono MP3 (this matches real production calls). Most phone recording apps output this by default.
  • Phone, not microphone: Use a real phone call, speakerphone, or WhatsApp voice call. Do not record in a silent room with a studio mic — that defeats the purpose.
  • Get creative with noise: Record from a busy road, an auto-rickshaw, a metro platform, a canteen, next to a fan or cooler. The messier the audio, the more useful the test.
  • Mix it up: Different accents, speaking speeds, volume levels. Have someone mumble. Talk over each other briefly. Switch between Hindi and English mid-sentence.
  • Scripts 3 and 4: Do not tell the evaluator which FATALs are in the script — we want to test blind detection.
  • You can improvise: Adapt names, numbers, and small details. Keep the structure and the embedded violations intact.

7. Transcription (STT) testing

For each combination, transcribe all 50 production calls + your role-play calls using the correct STT provider. Enable speaker diarisation. Label turns as Agent / Customer. You can use vendor dashboards, free-tier API keys, Python scripts, no-code tools — whatever works for you.

Reminder: If your team only does STT testing, that is a complete and valid submission.

Transcript quality — 1 to 5 scale

ScoreMeaning
5Near-perfect. Words, numbers, dates, amounts all correct. Speakers correctly separated.
4Minor errors that don't affect meaning. All key facts (amounts, dates, names) correct.
3Noticeable errors. Some facts garbled or speakers confused in places. A human could still score the call.
2Significant errors. Key facts wrong (e.g. ₹38,450 becomes ₹3,845). Scoring from this would be unreliable.
1Unusable. Large sections missing, wrong language, or speaker labels random.

Pay special attention to: numbers and amounts (must not be garbled), dates and deadlines (must not shift), Hinglish transitions (must not be dropped), speaker diarisation (must not swap Agent/Customer), and any transcription error that would cause a FATAL to be missed or false-flagged.

8. LLM scoring testing

What to do

For each combination and call: take the diarised transcript, provide the 19-parameter rubric (Section 4) as context, and ask the LLM to score: score per parameter (using the rubric's tiers), total out of 100, and FATAL flags with evidence. You may use any method — web UI, API, scripts, no-code tools.

Metrics — FATAL detection is primary

MetricWhat it measuresPriority
FATAL recall Of all human-flagged FATALs, what % did the LLM catch? PRIMARY
FATAL precision Of all LLM-flagged FATALs, what % were real (not false alarms)? HIGH
Non-FATAL accuracy Agreement rate with human scores on the 12 non-FATAL parameters MEDIUM
Total score MAE Mean absolute error of total score vs human baseline LOW
Decision rule

A combo with 100% FATAL recall and 70% non-FATAL accuracy is preferred over a combo with 80% FATAL recall and 95% non-FATAL accuracy. We optimise for safety first, precision second, cost third.

Scoring quality — 1 to 5 scale

  • 5 — Caught all FATALs, non-FATAL scores match human judgement, useful comments.
  • 4 — Caught all FATALs, minor non-FATAL disagreements, reasonable comments.
  • 3 — Missed 1 FATAL or raised 2+ false FATAL alarms.
  • 2 — Missed multiple FATALs or consistently wrong on non-FATALs.
  • 1 — Scores bear little relation to the actual call.

10. How Baysys.ai uses your work

Your qualitative feedback will be combined with internal quantitative metrics to select the production combo, identify parameters needing custom prompt engineering, and quantify cost at target volume (18,000 calls/day, ~60,000 audio minutes/day). A combo will only be chosen if it reliably catches FATALs and is trusted by people close to the collections and QA work.

11. Technical appendix

Quantitative metrics

  • STT: Word Error Rate (WER) overall and per language segment; entity accuracy for numbers, dates, and amounts; diarisation error rate vs hand-labelled segments.
  • FATAL detection: Recall, precision, and F1 per FATAL parameter type.
  • Non-FATAL scoring: Parameter-level agreement rate; Mean Absolute Error (MAE) of total score.
  • Operational: Throughput (audio minutes per real minute); error/timeout rates; daily cost projection at 60K min/day.

LLM scoring prompt guidance

When prompting the LLM, include the full 19-parameter rubric (with audit prompts from the canonical SCORECARD) in the system prompt, the diarised transcript as input, and instruct it to return structured JSON:

{
  "parameters": [
    {"id": 7, "name": "No harsh/threatening/abusive language",
     "group": "Call Quality", "score": 0, "max": 10,
     "fatal": true, "evidence": "Agent said: 'tum log paise khaate ho, saale'"}
  ],
  "total_score": 62,
  "fatal_violations": ["Abusive language", "RPV"],
  "score_band": "Needs Improvement"
}

Include this instruction: "If you are uncertain whether a FATAL violation occurred, flag it as FATAL. False alarms are preferable to missed violations."

The audit prompts in the scorecard (e.g. the de-escalation signal taxonomy for parameter 9) are designed to be included directly in LLM prompts.

Integration notes

  • Keep STT and scoring as separate modules — providers must be switchable via configuration.
  • The existing Call Auditor codebase has a provider-agnostic architecture and a placeholder model for custom LLM scoring — all hackathon combos fit within this structure.
  • Standardise scoring JSON for internal storage, even if vendors return different shapes.
  • Log full request/response payloads (PII-redacted) for debugging and audit.
